Why It Matters
Insulin is a key metabolic lever. When fasting insulin is elevated, your body is stuck in storage mode —
making it harder to burn fat, regulate appetite, and sustain energy between meals. Over time, elevated insulin
drives insulin resistance, which compounds metabolic dysfunction and increases the risk of type 2 diabetes,
cardiovascular disease, and chronic inflammation. We address it now because insulin is active — it’s the
main focus of this block.
What Happens If We Don't
Elevated insulin quietly works against you. Fat loss stalls, hunger becomes harder to manage, and energy
fluctuates throughout the day. Over time, the pancreas works harder to compensate, and the downstream
effects — weight gain, increased inflammation, hormonal disruption — become harder to reverse. We
address this early because the longer insulin stays elevated, the more entrenched the metabolic pattern
becomes.
How We’re Addressing It
Nutrition structure designed to reduce insulin demand — emphasizing protein, fiber, and healthy fats while
managing carbohydrate timing and quality. Berberine to directly improve insulin sensitivity at the cellular level.
Movement and meal timing strategies to support glucose clearance without relying on more insulin.

Why It Matters
ApoB counts the number of atherogenic particles in your blood — the vehicles that deposit cholesterol into
arterial walls. It’s a more accurate predictor of cardiovascular risk than LDL cholesterol alone. Even when
other markers look normal, elevated ApoB means your vascular system is under pressure. We address it now
because cardiovascular risk compounds silently, and ApoB responds well to targeted nutrition and lifestyle
intervention.
What Happens If We Don’t
Elevated ApoB is silent — you won’t feel it until significant damage is done. Plaque accumulates slowly but
relentlessly, narrowing arteries and increasing the risk of heart attack and stroke. Unlike inflammation or
insulin, there are no obvious symptoms to alert you. We address this now because the window for prevention
is wide open, and the interventions are straightforward.
How We’re Addressing It
Targeted nutrition to reduce atherogenic particle production — emphasizing soluble fiber to increase LDL
receptor activity, omega-3s to improve lipid metabolism, and dietary fat quality optimization. Your genetic
profile informs how aggressively we manage saturated fat intake and whether additional interventions may be
warranted.
Genetic Context: Lipoprotein & Cardiovascular Risk (ApoB)
ApoB reflects the number of atherogenic lipoprotein particles circulating in the blood. Particle burden is
shaped by how many particles are produced, how efficiently they are cleared, and how strongly dietary inputs
influence that balance.

CLINICAL ACTION SUMMARY
Your elevated ApoB level indicates a higher than optimal number of atherogenic particles in your
bloodstream, which is a primary driver of cardiovascular risk. Your genetic profile provides important context,
suggesting a tendency toward less efficient clearance and/or increased production of these particles, which
can raise your baseline risk when diet and lifestyle are not dialed in. The following strategies are designed to
directly address these underlying mechanisms.
• Manage Saturated Fat Intake: Your genetic risk profile may make your system more sensitive to the
LDL-raising effects of saturated fat. This type of fat can down-regulate the LDL receptors on your liver,
reducing its ability to clear ApoB-containing particles. Prioritize healthy fats from whole-food sources like
avocados, olive oil, nuts, and seeds, while being mindful of your intake from red meat, full-fat dairy, and
coconut oil.
• Increase Soluble Fiber: Soluble fiber is a powerful tool for lowering ApoB. It binds to bile acids in the gut,
forcing your liver to pull more cholesterol from the bloodstream to produce new bile acids. This directly
increases LDL receptor activity. Aim for at least 10 g/day of soluble fiber (and more if tolerated) from
sources like oats, barley, psyllium husk, apples, and beans.
• Consider Zone 2 Cardio: While all exercise is beneficial, steady-state cardiovascular exercise (often called
Zone 2) is particularly effective at improving mitochondrial function and the efficiency with which your
body uses fat for fuel. This can improve lipid metabolism and, alongside diet, contribute to lower levels of
circulating atherogenic particles over time. Incorporating regular sessions of 45-60 minutes at a
conversational pace is a sustainable way to support your cardiovascular health.
• Consider Plant Sterols: Foods fortified with plant sterols and stanols can be an effective adjunct strategy.
They work by blocking the absorption of cholesterol in the gut, which typically produces a modest
(roughly 5-10%) reduction in LDL cholesterol and ApoB when used consistently with other dietary
changes.
These genetic patterns do not determine cardiovascular risk on their own. They help explain why ApoB may rise more
easily or respond strongly to dietary inputs, particularly fat quality and fiber availability.

This section was triggered because you indicated that recovery limitations are a frequent concern. Your
genetic profile reveals specific tendencies in how your body manages stress, processes stimulants like
caffeine, and regulates your internal clock — all of which directly impact your ability to recover.
Why It Matters
Your recovery capacity is not just about rest — it’s a function of how your nervous system, circadian rhythm,
and stress-response pathways operate at the genetic level. Variants in genes like COMT, CYP1A2, PER3, and
FKBP5 can influence everything from how quickly you clear stress hormones to how efficiently you metabolize
caffeine and when your body naturally wants to sleep. Understanding these patterns allows us to build a

stress response that doesn’t downregulate efficiently — the effects cascade across every other system in your
body. Inflammation increases, insulin sensitivity decreases, and your body shifts from a state of repair to a
state of survival. Over time, this creates a compounding deficit that undermines training, body composition, and
cognitive performance.
How We’re Addressing It
Your protocol includes a targeted recovery strategy. This involves optimizing your sleep hygiene based on your
circadian tendencies, managing caffeine intake according to your metabolic profile, and implementing targeted
stress-reduction protocols that align with your neurological and cortisol-related genes. The goal is not to
eliminate stress, but to build a robust recovery infrastructure that allows your body to adapt and thrive. This
includes specific recommendations for sleep timing, light exposure, and daily decompression practices that are

Methylated B-Complex
Dose: 1 capsule daily (methylfolate + methylcobalamin)

Why It's Included
Methylated B-vitamins support the methylation cycle — a foundational process for DNA repair,

Look for products listing "methylfolate" (or 5-MTHF) and "methylcobalamin" — not "folic acid" or "cyanocobalamin".

Practical Notes
Titrate slowly — jumping to full dose causes bloating and gas. Take with plenty of water. Common sources: psyllium husk,
PHGG, oat beta-glucan, apple pectin. Best split across meals rather than taken all at once.
